# Digest Writing Guidelines

## Core Principles

1. **Report facts, not narratives** — State what happened. Avoid framing that implies a side.
2. **No loaded language** — Replace sensationalist words with neutral equivalents:
   - "slams" → "criticizes"
   - "bombshell" → "significant development"
   - "chaos" → "disruption" or describe specifically
   - "devastating" → describe the actual impact
   - "outrage" → "criticism" or "opposition"
3. **Multi-source corroboration** — If only one source reports something, note that. Prefer stories confirmed across multiple wire services.
4. **Attribution over assertion** — "According to AP" not "It is known that"
5. **Proportional coverage** — Don't let one dramatic story dominate unless it's genuinely the biggest development.

## Digest Structure

### Daily Digest Format

```
📰 Daily News Digest — [Date]

🌍 WORLD NEWS
• [Headline] — [1-2 sentence summary with attribution] (Sources: AP, BBC)
• [Headline] — ...

🇺🇸 NATIONAL NEWS
• [Headline] — [1-2 sentence summary with attribution] (Sources: Reuters, NPR)
• [Headline] — ...

📊 BY THE NUMBERS (optional)
• [Key statistic or data point from the day's news]
```

### Guidelines for Summaries

- **Lead with the 5 W's** — Who, What, When, Where, Why
- **One to two sentences max** per story
- **Include source attribution** in parentheses
- **Note when sources disagree** on facts or framing
- **Group related stories** (e.g., multiple stories about the same event)
- **Cap at 10-15 stories** — a digest, not an encyclopedia
- **Prioritize**: Wire service stories > public broadcaster > independent > political coverage

### What to Skip

- Opinion pieces (even from included sources)
- Celebrity/entertainment news (unless genuinely national significance)
- "Reactions to reactions" — meta-coverage of other media's coverage
- Stories sourced only from anonymous social media posts
- Clickbait-structured stories ("You won't believe...")

## Tone

Write like a Reuters wire dispatch: dry, factual, precise. The reader should not be able to tell the political leaning of the person who wrote the digest. If a story is politically charged, present both sides' stated positions without editorializing.
